Sleep is an essential function that allows your body and mind to recharge, leaving you refreshed and alert when you wake up. Healthy sleep also helps the body remain healthy and stave off diseases. Without enough sleep, the brain cannot function properly. This can impair your abilities to concentrate, think clearly, and process memories.

How Much Sleep Do I Need?

The amount of sleep each person needs varies. However, for most adults, 7 or more hours a night on a regular basis is recommended for optimal health. People who sleep less than 7 hours per night are more likely to report health problems than those who sleep 7 or more hours per night.

Recommended sleep by age:

- Newborns (0–3 months): 14–17 hours
- Infants (4–12 months): 12–16 hours per 24 hours
- Toddlers (1–2 years): 11–14 hours per 24 hours
- Preschoolers (3–5 years): 10–13 hours per 24 hours
- School-age children (6–12 years): 9–12 hours per 24 hours
- Teenagers (13–18 years): 8–10 hours per 24 hours
- Adults (18–60 years): 7 or more hours per night
- Adults (61–64 years): 7–9 hours
- Adults (65 years and older): 7–8 hours

What Happens During Sleep?

Sleep is not a passive state. Your brain and body are very active during sleep. The body cycles through different stages of sleep, each of which has specific functions:

Non-REM Stage 1 (NREM 1): Light sleep; the transition from wakefulness to sleep. Lasts 1–7 minutes. Heart rate and breathing begin to slow.

Non-REM Stage 2 (NREM 2): The body prepares for deep sleep. Body temperature drops, heart rate and breathing slow further. This is the longest sleep stage, comprising about 45% of total sleep time.

Non-REM Stage 3 (NREM 3): Deep sleep (also called slow-wave sleep). The most restorative stage. Growth hormone is released; the immune system is strengthened; tissue repair occurs. Hardest to wake from.

REM Sleep: Rapid Eye Movement sleep. Brain activity increases, dreaming occurs. Important for memory consolidation, learning, and emotional regulation. First REM period occurs about 90 minutes after falling asleep and becomes longer in later sleep cycles.

A typical adult cycles through these stages 4–6 times per night. Each cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes.

Sleep and Overall Health

Sufficient sleep is linked to:

Physical health: Sleep is involved in healing and repair of heart and blood vessels. Ongoing sleep deficiency is linked to increased risk of heart disease, kidney disease, high blood pressure, diabetes, and stroke.

Metabolism and weight: Sleep affects the hormones that control hunger (ghrelin) and fullness (leptin). Sleep deficiency leads to higher than normal levels of ghrelin and lower levels of leptin — increasing appetite and likelihood of overeating. Sleep-deprived people are also more likely to choose higher-calorie foods.

Immune function: During sleep, the immune system releases proteins called cytokines that help fight infection and inflammation. Sleep deprivation reduces cytokine production, impairing your body's ability to fight illness.

Mental health: Sleep plays a key role in brain function. Sleep deficiency is linked to depression, anxiety, impulsivity, and difficulty controlling emotions.

Safety: Drowsiness slows reaction time as much as drunk driving. Insufficient sleep is a leading cause of motor vehicle crashes and workplace accidents.

Growth and development: In children and teenagers, sleep is essential for growth, development, and learning. Growth hormone is primarily secreted during deep sleep.

Signs of Insufficient Sleep

You may not be getting enough sleep if you:
- Feel tired or drowsy during the day
- Need an alarm clock to wake up on time
- Have trouble concentrating or remembering things
- Feel irritable or moody
- Need caffeine to get through the day
- Fall asleep quickly when you sit down

Tips for Better Sleep

The CDC recommends the following sleep hygiene practices:

Be consistent: Go to bed at the same time each night and get up at the same time each morning, including on weekends and holidays.

Make sure your bedroom is quiet, dark, relaxing, and at a comfortable temperature: Use blackout curtains or a sleep mask; use earplugs or a white noise machine if necessary; keep the room cool (around 18°C/65°F is optimal for most adults).

Remove electronic devices from the bedroom: Televisions, computers, smartphones, and tablets emit blue light that signals the brain to stay awake, and can be sources of noise or distraction.

Avoid large meals, caffeine, and alcohol before bedtime: Caffeine and alcohol disrupt sleep cycles. Heavy meals close to bedtime can cause discomfort that disturbs sleep.

Get some exercise: Being physically active during the day can help you fall asleep more easily at night. Avoid vigorous exercise in the late evening, however, as this can make it harder to fall asleep.

When to See a Doctor

If you regularly have difficulty sleeping, consult a healthcare provider. Common treatable sleep disorders include:

Sleep apnoea: Breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep. Signs include loud snoring, waking with a gasping or choking sensation, and feeling tired despite a full night's sleep.

Insomnia: Persistent difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line recommended treatment.

Restless leg syndrome: Uncomfortable sensations in the legs that create an irresistible urge to move them, disrupting sleep.

Narcolepsy: Excessive daytime sleepiness and sudden muscle weakness, often triggered by emotion.

These conditions are highly treatable. Left untreated, they significantly impair health, quality of life, and weight management efforts.

Sleep and Weight Management

The connection between sleep and weight is bidirectional. Excess body weight — particularly when it causes sleep apnoea — disrupts sleep quality. Disrupted sleep, in turn, increases appetite and impairs the hormonal signals that regulate energy balance, promoting further weight gain.

Breaking this cycle requires addressing both sleep quality and body weight simultaneously. Modest weight loss (even 5–10% of body weight) can significantly improve sleep quality and reduce sleep apnoea severity. Simultaneously improving sleep supports the dietary and exercise efforts needed for weight loss.

Prioritising sleep as part of a weight management plan is not optional — it is as important as diet and exercise.
