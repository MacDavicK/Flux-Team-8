Strength training, also known as resistance training or weight training, involves exercising your muscles using resistance — either from weights, resistance bands, or your own bodyweight. It is one of the most effective forms of exercise for improving overall health, building muscle, increasing metabolism, and managing body weight.

Why Start Strength Training?

Strength training benefits people of all ages and fitness levels. You don't need to be young or already fit to start. The benefits include:

- Building and maintaining muscle mass (which naturally declines from your 30s onward)
- Increasing metabolism — muscle burns more calories at rest than fat, so more muscle means more calories burned throughout the day
- Strengthening bones, reducing osteoporosis risk
- Improving balance and reducing fall risk
- Managing chronic conditions including diabetes, arthritis, and heart disease
- Improving mood and mental health
- Improving sleep quality
- Increasing strength for everyday activities (lifting, carrying, climbing stairs)

Even modest amounts of strength training — two sessions per week of 20–30 minutes — can produce meaningful health improvements within 4–8 weeks of starting.

Getting Started: What You Need

One of the advantages of strength training is that you don't need expensive equipment to begin. You can start with:

Bodyweight exercises: Push-ups, squats, lunges, glute bridges, and planks require no equipment and are highly effective for building strength in beginners.

Resistance bands: Inexpensive and versatile, resistance bands can be used to target all major muscle groups and are ideal for home workouts.

Dumbbells: A pair of adjustable or fixed-weight dumbbells allows a wide variety of exercises and is a good first investment if you want home equipment.

Gym access: Gyms provide access to a variety of equipment — machines, barbells, dumbbells, and cables — and may offer guidance from fitness staff or trainers.

A Beginner's Strength Routine

For beginners, a full-body workout performed 2–3 times per week is recommended. This approach trains each muscle group frequently enough to stimulate adaptation while allowing adequate recovery between sessions.

Sample beginner full-body routine:

1. Squat (bodyweight or with dumbbells) — 3 sets of 10–12 reps
   Targets: quadriceps, hamstrings, glutes

2. Push-up (or dumbbell chest press) — 3 sets of 8–10 reps
   Targets: chest, shoulders, triceps

3. Dumbbell row or resistance band row — 3 sets of 10–12 reps per side
   Targets: back, biceps

4. Glute bridge or hip thrust — 3 sets of 12–15 reps
   Targets: glutes, hamstrings

5. Overhead dumbbell press — 3 sets of 10–12 reps
   Targets: shoulders, triceps

6. Plank — 3 sets of 20–30 seconds
   Targets: core

Rest 60–90 seconds between sets. Total workout time: approximately 30–40 minutes.

Key Principles for Safe and Effective Training

Warm up first: Before strength training, spend 5–10 minutes raising your heart rate with light aerobic activity (brisk walking, cycling, marching on the spot) and do some dynamic stretches for the muscles you'll be working. This reduces injury risk.

Focus on form: Correct technique is more important than the weight you lift. Poor form increases injury risk and reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. If unsure, watch reputable instructional videos or seek advice from a fitness professional.

Start lighter than you think you need: Beginners should start with a weight that allows them to complete all repetitions with good form without significant strain. Once you can comfortably complete the top of the rep range for all sets, increase the weight slightly.

Rest and recover: Muscles grow and get stronger during rest, not during the workout itself. Allow at least 48 hours between strength training sessions targeting the same muscle groups.

Progress gradually: Increase weight, repetitions, or sets gradually over time. Increasing too quickly is a common cause of injury.

Stay consistent: Results from strength training take time — most beginners notice improved strength in 4–6 weeks and visible muscle changes in 8–12 weeks with consistent training.

What to Expect as a Beginner

In the first 2–4 weeks, strength gains are primarily due to neurological adaptations — your nervous system learns to recruit muscle fibres more effectively. You will feel stronger before significant muscle mass changes are visible.

Muscle soreness (delayed onset muscle soreness, or DOMS) is normal after new exercises, particularly in the first few weeks. Soreness typically peaks 24–48 hours after exercise and resolves within 3–5 days. Light movement and gentle stretching can help manage soreness; complete rest is usually unnecessary.

After 8–12 weeks of consistent training, progress typically becomes more noticeable — increased muscle definition, improved posture, greater strength in daily activities, and improved energy levels.

Safety Considerations

- If you have any medical conditions (heart disease, high blood pressure, joint problems, diabetes, osteoporosis), consult your doctor or a qualified exercise professional before starting strength training
- Always use a spotter when lifting heavy weights alone
- If an exercise causes sharp or sudden pain (rather than the general discomfort of exertion), stop immediately and seek advice
- Stay hydrated during workouts
- Prioritise sleep and nutrition to support recovery and adaptation
