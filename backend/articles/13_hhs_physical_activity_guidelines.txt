The Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2nd Edition, provides evidence-based recommendations on physical activity, sedentary behaviour, and sleep for Americans across the lifespan. The guidelines are issued by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and represent the most comprehensive review of scientific evidence on physical activity and health ever conducted.

Key Messages

1. Regular physical activity is one of the most important things people can do to improve their health.
2. Moving more and sitting less will benefit nearly everyone.
3. Only a small fraction of Americans get the recommended amounts of physical activity.
4. Physical activity is safe for almost everyone, and the health benefits far outweigh the risks.
5. A range of activities counts — the focus should be on accumulating minutes and intensity rather than specific exercises.

Health Benefits of Regular Physical Activity

Strong evidence supports that regular physical activity:
- Reduces risk of all-cause mortality
- Reduces risk of cardiovascular disease death
- Reduces risk of cardiovascular disease, including heart disease and stroke
- Reduces risk of hypertension
- Reduces risk of type 2 diabetes
- Reduces risk of adverse blood lipid profile
- Reduces risk of cancers (bladder, breast, colon, endometrium, oesophagus, kidney, lung, and stomach)
- Prevents weight gain
- Improves cardiorespiratory and musculoskeletal fitness
- Reduces the risk of falls and fall-related injuries in older adults
- Reduces risk of depression and anxiety
- Improves sleep quality
- Improves cognitive function and reduces the risk of dementia

Recommendations for Adults (Ages 18–64)

Aerobic activity: Adults should move more and sit less throughout the day. Some physical activity is better than none. Adults who sit less and do any amount of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity gain some health benefits.

For substantial health benefits, adults should do:
- At least 150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity, OR
- 75 to 150 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity aerobic physical activity, OR
- An equivalent combination of both

Additional benefits are gained by engaging in physical activity beyond the equivalent of 300 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week.

Muscle-strengthening activities: Adults should also do muscle-strengthening activities of moderate or greater intensity that involve all major muscle groups on 2 or more days per week, as these activities provide additional health benefits.

Recommendations for Older Adults (Ages 65+)

The key guidelines for adults also apply to older adults. In addition, older adults should:
- Do multicomponent physical activity that includes balance training as well as aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities.
- Determine the level of effort for physical activity relative to their level of fitness.
- If chronic conditions or symptoms limit their ability to do 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity a week, they should be as physically active as their abilities and conditions allow.

Intensity Definitions

Moderate-intensity activities: cause a noticeable increase in heart rate and breathing but allow you to carry on a conversation (e.g., brisk walking, water aerobics, riding a bicycle on level ground, doubles tennis, ballroom dancing, general gardening).

Vigorous-intensity activities: cause large increases in heart rate and breathing that make it difficult to say more than a few words without pausing for breath (e.g., jogging or running, swimming laps, riding a bicycle fast or on hills, singles tennis, aerobic dancing, basketball, soccer, hiking uphill).

An easy way to gauge intensity: on a scale of 0 (sitting) to 10 (working as hard as possible), moderate-intensity is a 5–6, vigorous-intensity is a 7–8.

Muscle-Strengthening Activities

Muscle-strengthening activities, including resistance training and lifting weights, cause the body's muscles to work or hold against an applied force or weight. These activities involve a controlled set of movements and include:

- Exercises using weight machines or free weights
- Bodyweight exercises (push-ups, sit-ups, squats, lunges)
- Resistance band exercises
- Heavy gardening (digging, shovelling)
- Yoga
- Pilates

Muscle-strengthening activities count when they are performed at moderate or vigorous intensity. They should involve all major muscle groups: legs, hips, back, abdomen, chest, shoulders, and arms.

The minimum of 2 days per week of muscle-strengthening activity targets each major muscle group at least once. Additional sessions can be performed as fitness improves.

Reducing Sedentary Time

Sedentary behaviour — sitting, reclining, or lying down while awake — is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality, independent of whether guidelines for physical activity are met.

Even replacing sedentary time with light-intensity physical activity (standing, slow walking) produces measurable health benefits. Practical strategies:
- Take regular breaks from prolonged sitting (stand or walk briefly every 30–60 minutes)
- Use a standing desk for part of the workday
- Walk during phone calls
- Park further from destinations and walk the remainder
- Take stairs instead of lifts

Moving More, Starting Safely

For inactive adults, any amount of activity is beneficial. Begin with 10–15 minutes of light activity per day and gradually increase duration and intensity over weeks. Building up slowly reduces the risk of injury and allows the cardiovascular and musculoskeletal systems to adapt.
