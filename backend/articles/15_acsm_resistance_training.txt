Resistance training — also called strength training or weight training — is the use of resistance to muscular contraction to build the strength, anaerobic endurance, and size of skeletal muscles. The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) provides evidence-based guidelines for resistance training across the lifespan, including for beginners, intermediate exercisers, and advanced athletes.

Health and Fitness Benefits of Resistance Training

Resistance training offers a wide range of health and fitness benefits that complement those of aerobic exercise:

Increased muscle strength and endurance: Improves ability to perform daily activities, reduces injury risk, and enhances sports performance.

Increased lean body mass: Regular resistance training increases muscle mass, which raises resting metabolic rate and supports long-term weight management.

Reduced body fat: When combined with adequate nutrition, resistance training promotes fat loss while preserving lean tissue — an advantage over aerobic exercise alone during caloric restriction.

Improved bone mineral density: Mechanical loading of bone through resistance training stimulates bone formation and reduces osteoporosis risk.

Improved insulin sensitivity: Skeletal muscle is the primary site of glucose uptake; greater muscle mass improves glucose regulation and reduces type 2 diabetes risk.

Reduced blood pressure: Regular resistance training produces modest but clinically significant reductions in resting systolic and diastolic blood pressure.

Improved blood lipid profile: Associated with reductions in LDL cholesterol and triglycerides, and modest increases in HDL cholesterol.

Enhanced functional capacity: Particularly important in older adults, resistance training improves balance, mobility, and fall prevention.

Psychological benefits: Associated with reduced symptoms of depression and anxiety, improved self-efficacy, and better quality of life.

ACSM Resistance Training Recommendations

For general health and fitness in adults:

Frequency: Train each major muscle group 2–3 days per week, with at least 48 hours between sessions for the same muscle group.

Intensity:
- Novice and intermediate: 60–70% of one-repetition maximum (1RM)
- Experienced: 80–100% of 1RM for maximal strength gains
- Older adults, beginners, or those with health conditions: 40–50% of 1RM initially

Volume:
- Novice: 1–3 sets per exercise
- Intermediate: 3–4 sets per exercise
- Advanced: 4–6 sets per exercise

Repetitions:
- For muscular endurance: 15–25 repetitions at lighter loads
- For hypertrophy (muscle growth): 8–12 repetitions at moderate loads
- For maximal strength: 1–6 repetitions at heavy loads

Rest intervals:
- Muscular endurance: less than 30 seconds
- Hypertrophy: 30–90 seconds
- Strength: 2–5 minutes

Exercise selection: Include exercises that target all major muscle groups — legs (quadriceps, hamstrings, glutes, calves), back, chest, shoulders, biceps, triceps, and core.

Velocity: For novice exercisers, perform repetitions in a slow, controlled manner (2–4 seconds per phase). For intermediate and advanced, varying tempos can optimise different training goals.

Major Muscle Groups and Example Exercises

Legs:
- Quadriceps: squats, leg press, lunges, step-ups
- Hamstrings: deadlifts, Romanian deadlift, leg curls, good mornings
- Glutes: hip thrusts, glute bridges, deadlifts, squats
- Calves: calf raises (standing and seated)

Back:
- Latissimus dorsi: pull-ups/chin-ups, lat pulldowns, seated rows, single-arm dumbbell rows
- Trapezius and rhomboids: barbell rows, face pulls, upright rows

Chest:
- Bench press (flat, incline, decline), push-ups, chest flys, cable crossovers

Shoulders:
- Overhead press (barbell or dumbbell), lateral raises, front raises, rear delt flys

Arms:
- Biceps: bicep curls (barbell, dumbbell, cable), hammer curls
- Triceps: tricep dips, overhead extension, pushdowns

Core:
- Plank variations, dead bugs, bird dogs, ab wheel rollouts, hanging leg raises

Progressive Overload

The fundamental principle driving fitness gains in resistance training is progressive overload — gradually increasing the stress placed on the muscles over time. Without progressive challenge, the body adapts and progress plateaus.

Methods of progressive overload:
- Increase the weight lifted
- Increase the number of repetitions
- Increase the number of sets
- Decrease rest intervals
- Increase exercise frequency
- Alter the tempo (slower eccentric phase)

A general guideline: when you can comfortably complete the upper end of the target rep range for all sets, increase the load by 2–5% (smaller muscles) or 5–10% (larger muscles).

Getting Started Safely

Before beginning a resistance training programme, beginners should:
- Learn proper technique for each exercise — incorrect form increases injury risk
- Start with light loads (50–60% of estimated maximum) to develop motor patterns
- Perform 1–2 sets per exercise initially, progressing to 3 sets over several weeks
- Focus on compound (multi-joint) movements first, then accessory exercises
- Consider working with a qualified personal trainer or exercise professional initially

Most common beginner mistakes:
- Too much too soon (excessive volume or intensity)
- Poor exercise technique
- Neglecting certain muscle groups (commonly back, hamstrings, core)
- Insufficient rest and recovery
- Not tracking workouts (making progressive overload difficult)
