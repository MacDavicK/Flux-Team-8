Maintaining a healthy weight is important for your heart health. If you are overweight or obese, you are at higher risk of developing serious health problems, including heart disease, high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, gallstones, breathing problems, and certain cancers.

A healthy weight is determined using Body Mass Index (BMI). BMI is a measure of body fat based on height and weight that applies to adult men and women. A BMI of 18.5 to 24.9 is considered healthy. A BMI of 25.0 to 29.9 is considered overweight. A BMI of 30.0 or higher is considered obese.

To achieve and maintain a healthy weight, balance the calories you eat and drink with the calories you burn. Calorie balance refers to the relationship between calories consumed from foods and beverages and calories expended through physical activity and metabolic processes.

Key Strategies for Healthy Weight Loss

Focus on gradual, steady weight loss of 0.5 to 1 kilogram (1 to 2 pounds) per week. Rapid weight loss can lead to muscle loss, nutritional deficiencies, and gallstones.

Create a calorie deficit of 500 to 1,000 calories per day through a combination of eating fewer calories and increasing physical activity. This deficit will typically produce 0.5 to 1 kg of weight loss per week.

Eat a variety of nutritious foods. Choose fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein sources, and low-fat dairy. These foods provide essential nutrients while helping you feel full on fewer calories.

Limit foods high in calories, saturated fat, and added sugar. These include sugary drinks, fast food, candy, and processed snacks. Empty calories contribute to weight gain without providing nutritional benefit.

Physical Activity Recommendations

Adults should get at least 150 to 300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, or 75 to 150 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity per week for substantial health benefits. For weight loss or preventing weight regain, more activity — up to 300 minutes or more per week — may be needed.

Muscle-strengthening activities that work all major muscle groups should be done on 2 or more days per week. Building muscle increases your resting metabolic rate, helping you burn more calories even at rest.

Start slowly and gradually increase activity levels. Even small increases in physical activity can have meaningful health benefits.

Behavioural Approaches

Keep a food and activity journal. People who track what they eat and how much they move consistently lose more weight and maintain weight loss better than those who do not.

Set specific, realistic goals. Instead of "I want to lose weight," try "I will walk 30 minutes every day after dinner and eat vegetables at every meal."

Identify and address triggers for overeating. Stress, boredom, and social situations can all lead to eating more than intended. Developing healthy coping strategies reduces emotional eating.

Seek support. Having a support network of friends, family, or a healthcare professional significantly improves long-term success.

Weight Maintenance

After reaching a healthy weight, maintaining it requires continued attention to healthy eating and regular physical activity. Most people who successfully maintain weight loss:

- Eat breakfast every day
- Weigh themselves regularly (at least weekly)
- Watch less than 10 hours of television per week
- Exercise about 1 hour per day (usually walking)
- Follow a consistent eating pattern on weekdays and weekends
- Limit fast food consumption to less than once per week

Weight maintenance is often harder than weight loss. Planning for how to handle setbacks and high-risk situations (holidays, vacations, stressful periods) is an important part of long-term success.
