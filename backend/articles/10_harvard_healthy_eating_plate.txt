Category: nutrition | Authority: university
---
The Healthy Eating Plate, created by nutrition experts at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health and editors at Harvard Health Publications, was designed to address deficiencies in the United States Department of Agriculture's MyPlate guide. It provides detailed guidance to help people make the best eating choices.

The Healthy Eating Plate is based on the best available science and was not influenced by the food industry or agricultural policy.

Make Most of Your Meal Vegetables and Fruits — ½ of Your Plate

Aim for colour and variety, and remember that potatoes and chips don't count as vegetables on the Healthy Eating Plate because of their negative impact on blood sugar.

Vegetables are rich in vitamins, minerals, fibre, and phytochemicals. Eating a wide variety of coloured vegetables helps ensure a diverse intake of nutrients. Dark leafy greens (kale, spinach, bok choy), red and orange vegetables (tomatoes, sweet peppers, carrots), and cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts) are among the most nutrient-dense options.

Fruits provide natural sugars along with fibre, vitamins C and A, potassium, and antioxidants. Whole fruits are preferred over fruit juices because juice removes most of the fibre and concentrates the sugars.

Go for Whole Grains — ¼ of Your Plate

Whole and intact grains — whole wheat, barley, wheat berries, quinoa, oats, brown rice, and foods made with them, such as whole wheat pasta — have a milder effect on blood sugar and insulin than white bread, white rice, and other refined grains.

The fibre in whole grains slows digestion, helps you feel full longer, and reduces the insulin response. Research consistently links higher whole grain intake to lower risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and some cancers.

Practical swaps:
- Brown or wild rice instead of white rice
- Whole wheat bread or pasta instead of refined grain versions
- Oatmeal instead of sugary breakfast cereals
- Quinoa as a side dish instead of white pasta

Protein Power — ¼ of Your Plate

Fish, poultry, beans, and nuts are all healthy, versatile protein sources — they can be mixed into salads, and pair well with vegetables on a plate. Limit red meat and avoid processed meats such as bacon and sausage.

Evidence from the Harvard Nurses' Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study found that higher intake of red and processed meats is associated with elevated risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers.

Protein quality considerations:
- Fish (especially fatty fish like salmon, sardines, and mackerel) provides omega-3 fatty acids with demonstrated cardiovascular benefits
- Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas) provide protein along with fibre and are associated with reduced chronic disease risk
- Processed meats (hot dogs, sausage, deli meats) contain preservatives such as nitrates and nitrites associated with increased colorectal cancer risk

Healthy Plant Oils — In Moderation

Use olive oil, canola, soy, corn, sunflower, peanut, and other plant oils in cooking, on salad, and at the table. Limit butter and avoid trans fat.

Olive oil, in particular, is rich in monounsaturated fatty acids and polyphenols with anti-inflammatory properties. The Mediterranean diet's heavy reliance on olive oil is thought to contribute significantly to its cardiovascular protective effects.

Oils to limit or avoid:
- Coconut oil: High in saturated fat despite popular claims of benefits; evidence does not support preferring it over unsaturated options
- Butter and animal fats: Use sparingly
- Partially hydrogenated oils (trans fats): Avoid entirely; banned in many countries due to clear cardiovascular harm

Drink Water, Coffee, or Tea

Drink water, tea, or coffee (with little or no sugar), and limit milk/dairy (1–2 servings per day) and juice (1 small glass per day). Avoid sugary drinks.

Water is the ideal hydration choice: it has no calories, no additives, and supports all body functions. Coffee and tea provide antioxidants and, in moderate quantities, are associated with reduced risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and some cancers.

Milk and dairy: While dairy is a convenient source of calcium, it is not essential for adults who include adequate calcium from other sources. Two servings per day (e.g., one cup of milk and one small yoghurt) is a reasonable ceiling.

Sugary beverages — sodas, sports drinks, energy drinks, and sweetened coffee drinks — are one of the most significant contributors to excess calorie intake in modern diets and are associated with weight gain, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease.

Stay Active

The Healthy Eating Plate also recognises that diet alone is insufficient for optimal health. The red running figure on the plate reminds people that staying active is also important for weight control.

Regular physical activity:
- Burns calories and supports energy balance
- Preserves lean muscle mass during weight loss
- Improves insulin sensitivity and metabolic health
- Reduces risk of chronic disease independent of body weight
- Supports mental health and cognitive function

The Harvard recommendation aligns with national guidelines: at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week, with additional benefits from greater amounts.

What the Healthy Eating Plate Does Not Emphasise

Unlike some other dietary guides, the Healthy Eating Plate does not recommend a specific dairy intake, does not include processed grain products prominently, and explicitly does not endorse potatoes as a vegetable serving. These positions reflect the accumulated epidemiological evidence rather than food industry or agricultural interests.
