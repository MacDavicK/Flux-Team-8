Achieving and maintaining a healthy weight is a long-term process that requires adopting sustainable habits around food, physical activity, and mindset. The following guidelines are grounded in nutritional science and designed to help individuals make meaningful, lasting changes.

Understanding Energy Balance

Weight management is fundamentally about energy balance: the relationship between the calories you consume and the calories your body burns. To lose weight, you need to create a caloric deficit — consuming fewer calories than you expend over time.

However, the goal is not just to eat less, but to eat well. Nutritional quality matters alongside quantity. A diet sufficient in vitamins, minerals, protein, fibre, and healthy fats supports metabolic health, energy levels, and the sustainability of weight loss efforts.

Estimating Your Calorie Needs

Your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) depends on:
- Basal metabolic rate (BMR): the calories your body burns at rest
- Physical activity level: sedentary, lightly active, moderately active, very active
- Thermic effect of food: approximately 10% of calories consumed are used in digestion

General TDEE estimates:
- Sedentary adult (little to no exercise): BMR × 1.2
- Lightly active (1–3 days/week exercise): BMR × 1.375
- Moderately active (3–5 days/week exercise): BMR × 1.55
- Very active (6–7 days/week exercise): BMR × 1.725

To create a deficit for gradual weight loss, subtract 500 calories from your estimated TDEE. This typically produces 0.5 kg (1 pound) of fat loss per week.

Building a Balanced Plate

A practical framework for building meals that support weight loss:

Half the plate: non-starchy vegetables
- Leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, peppers, cucumbers, zucchini, tomatoes
- These provide fibre, micronutrients, and high satiety for few calories

One quarter: lean protein
- Chicken breast, turkey, fish, eggs, tofu, legumes, low-fat cottage cheese
- Aim for 20–35 g of protein per meal

One quarter: complex carbohydrates
- Brown rice, quinoa, sweet potato, oats, whole grain bread or pasta
- Choose minimally refined sources that retain fibre and nutrients

Plus: small amount of healthy fat
- Olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds
- Fat supports satiety and the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins

Practical Strategies to Reduce Calorie Intake Without Deprivation

Eat slowly: It takes approximately 20 minutes for satiety signals to reach the brain. Eating slowly and mindfully allows you to recognise fullness before overeating.

Choose high-volume, low-calorie foods: Non-starchy vegetables, broth-based soups, fruits with high water content, and whole grains provide satiety with relatively few calories compared with energy-dense processed foods.

Don't drink your calories: Sugary beverages, alcoholic drinks, specialty coffees, and fruit juices can add hundreds of calories per day without providing meaningful satiety. Prioritise water, unsweetened tea, and black coffee.

Reduce portion sizes gradually: Drastic changes are hard to maintain. Reducing portions by 10–20% per meal is a manageable starting point that accumulates to meaningful caloric reduction over time.

Meal prep and planning: Preparing meals in advance reduces reliance on high-calorie convenience foods. Having healthy options readily available makes healthier choices the path of least resistance.

Limit eating out: Restaurant meals are typically 30–50% higher in calories than home-cooked meals. When eating out, choose grilled over fried options, ask for dressings and sauces on the side, and be mindful of portion sizes.

Micronutrient Considerations During Weight Loss

Caloric restriction can create risks of nutritional deficiency if not managed carefully. Key nutrients to prioritise:

Iron: Found in lean red meat, legumes, fortified cereals, and leafy greens. Iron deficiency is common, particularly in women, and causes fatigue that undermines physical activity.

Calcium: Found in dairy products, fortified plant milks, tofu, and leafy greens. Important for bone health and may play a role in fat metabolism.

Vitamin D: Many people are deficient, particularly in low-sunlight environments. Found in fatty fish, egg yolks, fortified foods, and sunlight exposure.

B vitamins: Essential for energy metabolism. Found in whole grains, legumes, eggs, meat, and dairy. Those on very restrictive diets may need supplementation.

Omega-3 fatty acids: Found in fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts. Support cardiovascular health and may reduce inflammation associated with obesity.

Special Considerations

Emotional eating: Many people eat in response to stress, boredom, or negative emotions rather than true hunger. Recognising emotional eating triggers and developing alternative coping strategies is an important part of long-term success.

Mindful eating: Paying full attention to food — the taste, texture, and experience of eating — without distraction can reduce overall intake and improve satisfaction.

Consistency over perfection: A consistent, moderate approach to dietary change produces better long-term outcomes than cycling between restriction and over-indulgence. Small improvements maintained over months and years compound into significant health gains.
