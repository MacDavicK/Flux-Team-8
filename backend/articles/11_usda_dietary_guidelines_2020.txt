The Dietary Guidelines for Americans, 2020–2025 provides advice on what to eat and drink to meet nutrient needs, promote health, and prevent disease. For the first time, this edition provides guidance for every life stage, from birth through older adulthood, including women who are pregnant or lactating.

The Overarching Guidelines

The 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans provides four overarching guidelines:

1. Follow a healthy dietary pattern at every life stage.
2. Customise and enjoy nutrient-dense food and beverage choices to reflect personal preferences, cultural traditions, and budgetary considerations.
3. Focus on meeting food group needs with nutrient-dense foods and beverages, and stay within calorie limits.
4. Limit foods and beverages higher in added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium, and limit alcoholic beverages.

Core Elements of a Healthy Dietary Pattern

Vegetables of all types — dark green; red and orange; beans, peas, and lentils; starchy; and other vegetables.

Fruits, especially whole fruit.

Grains, at least half of which are whole grain.

Dairy, including fat-free or low-fat milk, yoghurt, and cheese, and/or lactose-free versions and fortified soy beverages and yoghurt as alternatives.

Protein foods, including lean meats, poultry, and eggs; seafood; beans, peas, and lentils; and nuts, seeds, and soy products.

Oils, including vegetable oils and oils in food, such as seafood and nuts.

Limits on Added Sugars

The Dietary Guidelines recommend consuming less than 10% of calories per day from added sugars starting at age 2. Avoid foods and beverages with added sugars for those younger than 2 years.

In a 2,000-calorie diet, the 10% limit on added sugars amounts to no more than 200 calories from added sugars — roughly 12 teaspoons (50 grams) per day.

Foods and beverages that are major sources of added sugars:
- Sweetened beverages (sodas, sports drinks, energy drinks, fruit drinks)
- Desserts and sweet snacks (cakes, cookies, ice cream)
- Sweetened coffee and tea drinks
- Candy and sugar

Consuming less than 10% of calories from added sugars aligns with evidence linking lower added sugar intake to reduced risk of overweight and obesity, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes.

Limits on Saturated Fat

Consume less than 10% of calories per day from saturated fat. In a 2,000-calorie diet, this is no more than about 22 grams of saturated fat per day.

Foods high in saturated fat include: fatty cuts of meat and poultry, full-fat dairy products (whole milk, butter, cream, cheese), and tropical oils (coconut oil, palm oil).

The evidence supports replacing saturated fat with unsaturated fat (especially polyunsaturated fat) to reduce LDL cholesterol levels and risk of cardiovascular disease.

Limits on Sodium

Consume less than 2,300 milligrams of sodium per day for individuals 14 years of age and older. This is approximately 1 teaspoon of table salt per day.

More than 70% of American sodium intake comes from processed, packaged, and restaurant foods — not from salt added at the table or in cooking. Foods high in sodium include: canned soups and vegetables, processed meats (deli meat, bacon, hot dogs), pizza, bread and rolls, fast food, and condiments.

High sodium intake is causally linked to high blood pressure (hypertension), which is the leading risk factor for stroke and a major risk factor for heart disease.

Beverages and Alcohol

Water is recommended as the primary beverage for hydration. The guidelines recommend that if alcohol is consumed, it should be in moderation — up to one drink per day for women and up to two drinks per day for men — and only by adults of legal drinking age.

Alcohol should be avoided during pregnancy and is not recommended for individuals younger than 21 years.

Caffeinated beverages including coffee and tea are safe for most adults in moderate amounts (up to 400 mg caffeine per day) and are associated with some health benefits.

The Role of Nutrient Density

The concept of "nutrient density" is central to the 2020–2025 guidelines. Nutrient-dense foods and beverages provide vitamins, minerals, and other health-promoting components with relatively few calories and limited amounts of saturated fat, sodium, and added sugars.

Examples of highly nutrient-dense foods: leafy greens, vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, low-fat dairy, lean proteins, seafood, nuts, and seeds.

Examples of foods with low nutrient density: sweetened beverages, candy, snack chips, pastries, and other ultra-processed foods.

The guidelines emphasise that all foods can fit within a healthy dietary pattern — the overall pattern is what matters most, not the inclusion or exclusion of any single food.

Practical Application

Meeting the guidelines within a 2,000-calorie dietary pattern includes approximately:
- 2½ cups of vegetables daily
- 2 cups of fruit daily
- 6 oz equivalent of grains (at least 3 oz whole grains) daily
- 3 cups of dairy daily
- 5½ oz equivalent of protein foods daily
- 27 g of oils daily

These targets should be adapted to individual calorie needs, life stage, physical activity level, and health goals.
