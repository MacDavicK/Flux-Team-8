Title: Steps for Losing Weight | Source: https://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/losing_weight/index.html
Category: weight_loss | Authority: government
---
When it comes to losing weight, there's no shortage of advice. But lasting weight loss requires making lifestyle changes you can maintain over time. The CDC recommends a gradual, steady approach to weight loss of 0.5 to 1 kg (1 to 2 pounds) per week.

Even a small amount of weight loss — 5 to 10 percent of your total body weight — is likely to produce health benefits such as improvements in blood pressure, blood cholesterol, and blood sugar.

Step 1: Make a Commitment

Before starting a weight-loss program, make a genuine commitment. Managing your weight requires long-term effort. Consider what meaningful changes you can make to your eating and physical activity habits.

Think about what has prevented successful weight loss in the past. Understanding past barriers helps you plan better strategies this time.

Step 2: Take Stock of Where You Are

Keep a food diary for a week. Write down everything you eat and drink, the time, the portion size, and your mood. This helps identify patterns — such as eating out of boredom or skipping meals that lead to overeating later.

Track your physical activity. Note how much time you spend sitting versus being active.

Step 3: Set Realistic Goals

Set both short-term process goals and long-term outcome goals. Process goals focus on behaviours (e.g., "I will walk for 30 minutes on 5 days per week"). Outcome goals focus on results (e.g., "I will lose 10 kg").

Make goals SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-limited. Vague goals lead to vague results.

Step 4: Identify Resources for Information and Support

Talk with your healthcare provider. A doctor, nurse, or dietitian can help you set appropriate goals and identify any medical considerations.

Find a buddy. Social support matters. Consider joining a weight management group or enlisting a friend or family member.

Step 5: Continuously Monitor Your Progress

Weigh yourself regularly. Most successful weight managers weigh themselves at least once a week, ideally at the same time each day (morning, after using the bathroom).

Continue keeping a food and activity journal. Studies consistently show that self-monitoring is one of the strongest predictors of successful weight loss.

Reassess your goals regularly. As you progress, your initial goals may become easier to achieve. Update them to keep making progress.

Calorie Considerations

To lose weight, you need to eat fewer calories than your body uses. Most adults need to reduce intake by 500 to 1,000 calories per day to lose about 0.5 to 1 kg per week.

However, do not cut calories too drastically. Eating fewer than 1,200 calories per day (for women) or 1,500 calories per day (for men) can be harmful and is difficult to sustain.

Focus on reducing calorie-dense foods rather than eliminating entire food groups. Small changes add up: replacing a sugary drink with water saves 150 calories or more; choosing a small portion of nuts instead of crisps saves 100 calories.

Physical Activity for Weight Loss

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (such as brisk walking) per week. For greater weight loss, aim for 300 minutes per week.

Add muscle-strengthening activities at least 2 days per week. Muscle burns more calories at rest than fat, which helps with weight maintenance.

Reduce sedentary time. Even breaking up long periods of sitting with brief walks or standing can contribute to better health outcomes.

Dietary Changes for Weight Loss

Reduce portion sizes. Use smaller plates, measure serving sizes, and avoid eating directly from large packages.

Eat more fruits and vegetables. They are nutrient-dense and relatively low in calories, helping you feel full.

Choose whole grains over refined grains. Whole grains provide more fibre, which promotes satiety.

Limit sugar-sweetened beverages. Liquid calories do not satisfy hunger the way solid food does, making it easy to consume excess calories from drinks.

Plan meals in advance. Having healthy foods readily available reduces the likelihood of making poor food choices when hungry.
