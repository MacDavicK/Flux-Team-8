Title: Water: How Much Should You Drink Every Day? | Source: https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/nutritionsource/water/
Category: nutrition | Authority: university
---
Water is essential to good health. Every system in the body depends on water. Water flushes toxins out of vital organs, carries nutrients to cells, and provides a moist environment for ear, nose, and throat tissues. A lack of water can lead to dehydration, a condition that occurs when you don't have enough water in your body to carry out normal functions. Even mild dehydration can drain your energy and make you tired.

How Much Water Do You Need?

Every day you lose water through your breath, perspiration, urine, and bowel movements. For your body to function properly, you must replenish its water supply by consuming beverages and foods that contain water.

The U.S. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine determined that an adequate daily fluid intake is:

- About 3.7 litres (125 oz or about 15½ cups) of fluids per day for men
- About 2.7 litres (91 oz or about 11½ cups) of fluids per day for women

These recommendations cover fluids from water, other beverages, and food. About 20% of daily fluid intake usually comes from food, and the rest from beverages.

Factors That Affect Water Needs

Exercise: Any activity that makes you sweat requires you to replenish fluid. Drink water before, during, and after a workout. If exercise is intense and lasts more than an hour, a sports drink with electrolytes can help replace minerals in your blood.

Environment: Hot or humid weather causes you to sweat, requiring additional fluid intake. Heated indoor air also can cause your skin to lose moisture during winter.

Overall health: Your body loses fluids when you have a fever, vomiting, or diarrhoea. Drink more water or follow a doctor's recommendation to drink oral rehydration solutions. Conditions like bladder infections and urinary tract stones also require additional water.

Pregnancy and breast-feeding: If you're pregnant or breast-feeding, you may need additional fluids.

Water and Weight Management

Water has no calories and can help with weight management in several ways:

Appetite suppression: Drinking water before meals can help reduce appetite. Research suggests that drinking about 500 ml (two glasses) of water 30 minutes before a meal can reduce calorie intake at that meal, particularly in older adults.

Increased energy expenditure: Drinking cold water may slightly increase metabolic rate as the body works to warm the water to body temperature. The effect is modest (approximately 24–30% increase in metabolic rate for 1 hour after drinking cold water), but cumulative over time.

Replacement of caloric beverages: When water replaces sugary drinks, calorie intake decreases substantially. Swapping just one 350 ml soda per day with water reduces annual calorie intake by approximately 51,000 calories — equivalent to about 6–7 kg of body fat.

Improved exercise performance: Even mild dehydration (1–2% of body weight) can reduce aerobic exercise performance, leading to lower calorie burn during workouts.

Do You Need 8 Glasses a Day?

You've probably heard the advice to drink eight 8-ounce glasses of water a day (about 2 litres, or "8x8"). This is easy to remember and is a reasonable goal. The actual evidence base for this specific number is weak — individual needs vary considerably based on body size, activity level, climate, and diet.

The best approach is to monitor your urine colour:
- Pale yellow (like lemonade): well hydrated
- Dark yellow or amber: drink more water
- Colourless/clear: possibly overhydrated (rare risk unless consuming large volumes rapidly)

Sources of Hydration

Most healthy people can stay hydrated by drinking water and other fluids throughout the day. Here are some options:

Water: The best overall choice. No calories, no additives, universally available.

Sparkling water and mineral water: Good alternatives to plain water for those who prefer carbonation.

Coffee and tea: Contribute to daily fluid intake and contain beneficial antioxidants. In moderate amounts (up to 4 cups of coffee per day), they are not associated with dehydration despite containing caffeine.

Milk: Provides fluid along with protein, calcium, and vitamin D.

Fruit juices and sports drinks: Provide fluids but also significant calories and sugars. Use sparingly or when electrolyte replacement is genuinely needed (prolonged intense exercise).

Fruits and vegetables: Foods with high water content — cucumber (96% water), lettuce (95%), celery (95%), zucchini (94%), tomatoes (94%), watermelon (92%), strawberries (91%) — contribute significantly to daily fluid intake.

Hydration and Kidney Health

Adequate water intake reduces the risk of kidney stones by diluting the concentration of minerals in urine that can crystallise and form stones. A daily fluid intake of at least 2 litres is recommended for people at risk of calcium oxalate kidney stones.

When to Drink More

Signs that you may need to increase your water intake:
- Persistent fatigue without clear cause
- Headaches (often dehydration-related)
- Difficulty concentrating
- Dry mouth or dry skin
- Infrequent urination or very dark urine
- Constipation

During hot weather, during illness with fever or gastrointestinal symptoms, and during and after exercise, proactively increasing fluid intake is important rather than waiting until thirst signals appear, particularly in older adults whose thirst sensation may be diminished.
