Evidence-based physical activity recommendations represent the scientific consensus on the types, frequency, duration, and intensity of exercise needed to produce meaningful health and fitness benefits. Part 2 of this series focuses on resistance training and muscular fitness — the evidence base for recommendations, how to apply them, and common questions from practitioners and the public.

The Scientific Basis for Resistance Training Recommendations

The current evidence base for resistance training recommendations draws from:

- Dose-response studies: Establishing minimum effective doses and the point of diminishing returns for various health outcomes
- Randomised controlled trials: Comparing different training parameters (frequency, volume, intensity) in defined populations
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses: Pooling results across multiple studies to identify robust effects

This evidence forms the foundation for recommendations from the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM), the American Heart Association (AHA), the World Health Organization (WHO), and national physical activity guidelines.

Frequency: How Often Should You Train?

The evidence supports training each major muscle group 2–3 days per week for most health and fitness goals. More frequent training (4+ days per week) can produce additional benefits for advanced individuals but is not necessary and increases injury risk in untrained populations.

Key findings on frequency:
- Once per week produces meaningful initial strength gains in beginners
- Twice per week is sufficient for maintaining strength gains once achieved
- Three times per week produces optimal hypertrophy and strength gains for most non-athletic goals
- Greater frequency (4+ days) provides marginal additional benefit when total volume is equated

A minimum of 48 hours between sessions targeting the same muscle group is strongly supported by the evidence to allow adequate recovery and adaptation.

Volume: How Much Should You Do?

Volume is typically quantified as the total number of sets per muscle group per week. Evidence suggests:

- Beginners: 10–15 total sets per muscle group per week is adequate for substantial strength and hypertrophy gains
- Intermediate: 15–20 sets per muscle group per week for continued progress
- Advanced: up to 25 sets per muscle group per week (with appropriate periodisation)

Research suggests a dose-response relationship for hypertrophy up to approximately 10 hard sets per session per muscle group, above which recovery is compromised.

The minimum effective volume for maintaining muscle mass is remarkably low — as few as 1–2 sets per muscle group per week can maintain gains previously achieved. This is important context for busy periods or travel.

Intensity: How Heavy?

Intensity refers to the percentage of the one-repetition maximum (1RM) used during training. The evidence has evolved significantly on this:

Traditional view: Heavy loads (70–85% of 1RM) were considered necessary for hypertrophy and strength.

Current evidence: Loads across a wide range (30–90% of 1RM) can produce similar hypertrophy when sets are performed to or near muscular failure. The effort applied (proximity to failure) matters more than the absolute load.

Practical implications:
- For beginners: Moderate loads (60–70% of 1RM) with controlled technique are appropriate
- For those unable to train with heavy loads (older adults, those with joint issues): Lighter loads performed to near-failure are equally effective for hypertrophy
- For strength gains: Heavier loads (80%+ of 1RM) produce superior strength outcomes due to neurological adaptations

Rest Periods: How Long Between Sets?

Longer rest periods (3–5 minutes) produce superior strength and power adaptations. Shorter rest periods (60–90 seconds) increase metabolic stress and may enhance hypertrophy in some contexts.

Practical recommendation: Rest 90–120 seconds between sets for most goals. This balances recovery, training efficiency, and adaptation stimulus.

Exercise Order

Evidence supports:
- Performing multi-joint compound exercises (squats, bench press, rows) before single-joint isolation exercises (curls, extensions)
- Training larger muscle groups or priority areas earlier in the session when energy and focus are highest
- Alternating upper and lower body exercises in circuit-style training is an effective time-efficient option

Progressive Overload: The Fundamental Principle

No aspect of resistance training is more evidence-based than the principle of progressive overload. Muscles adapt to a training stimulus; to continue improving, the stimulus must be progressively increased over time.

Evidence-based approaches to progression:
- Linear periodisation: Gradually increasing load over weeks (most appropriate for beginners)
- Undulating periodisation: Varying intensity and volume within the week (more appropriate for intermediate/advanced)
- Double progression: Increase reps within a target range before increasing load (e.g., perform 3 sets of 8–12; when all sets reach 12, increase load to bring reps back to 8)

Without progressive overload, adaptation plateaus within 6–8 weeks as the body accommodates to the training stimulus.

Special Populations: Evidence-Based Considerations

Older adults (65+):
- Resistance training is safe and highly effective in older adults, even those over 80
- Focus on functional movements (squats, deadlifts, step-ups) that improve daily living activities
- Include balance training alongside resistance training to prevent falls
- Higher-repetition, lower-load training (15–20 reps) may be more joint-friendly while still producing adaptation

Women:
- Women respond to resistance training in qualitatively similar ways to men
- Women have the same capacity for relative strength gains and hypertrophy per unit of muscle
- Women tend to recover faster from training sessions than men, potentially supporting higher training frequencies
- Hormonal fluctuations across the menstrual cycle affect recovery; adjusting training intensity accordingly may be beneficial

Summary of Minimum Effective Dose

For health benefits with minimal time investment:
- Frequency: 2 days per week
- Volume: 1–3 sets per exercise, targeting all major muscle groups
- Intensity: 60–70% of 1RM, or a weight that allows 10–15 repetitions with good form but is challenging
- Duration: 20–30 minutes per session

This minimal dose is sufficient to produce meaningful improvements in strength, muscle mass, bone density, metabolic health, and functional capacity in previously untrained individuals.
