Physical activity is defined as any bodily movement produced by skeletal muscles that requires energy expenditure. Physical activity refers to all movement including during leisure time, for transport to get to and from places, or as part of a person's work. Both moderate- and vigorous-intensity physical activity improve health.

"Physical inactivity" is now identified as the fourth leading risk factor for global mortality, causing an estimated 3.2 million deaths globally.

The Global Problem of Physical Inactivity

Physical inactivity is a growing public health concern. Globally, 1 in 4 adults does not meet global recommendations for physical activity. More than 80% of the world's adolescent population is insufficiently physically active. Insufficient physical activity is one of the leading risk factors for death worldwide and is on the rise in many countries, increasing the risks of noncommunicable diseases (NCDs) and affecting general health worldwide.

Health Benefits of Physical Activity

Regular physical activity of moderate intensity — such as walking, cycling, or doing sports — has significant benefits for health. At all ages, the benefits of being physically active outweigh the potential harm, for example through accidents.

Some physical activity is better than doing none. By becoming more active throughout the day in relatively simple ways, people can quite easily achieve the recommended activity levels.

Regular physical activity helps to:
- Prevent and manage heart diseases, diabetes, cancer, and stroke
- Reduce symptoms of depression and anxiety
- Reduce cognitive decline, dementia, and Alzheimer's disease
- Maintain healthy weight and prevent obesity
- Improve overall well-being
- Strengthen muscles and bones
- Improve the ability to do everyday activities and prevent falls in older adults

Recommended Levels of Physical Activity for Different Age Groups

Children and adolescents (5–17 years):
- Should do at least an average of 60 minutes per day of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity, mainly aerobic
- Should include vigorous-intensity aerobic, as well as those that strengthen muscle and bone, at least 3 days a week
- Should limit the amount of time spent being sedentary

Adults (18–64 years):
- At least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity
- Or at least 75–150 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic physical activity
- Or an equivalent combination throughout the week
- Muscle-strengthening activities at moderate or greater intensity, involving all major muscle groups, on 2 or more days a week
- Limiting sedentary time and replacing it with physical activity of any intensity

Adults (65 years and above):
- Same as adults (above), plus:
- As part of their weekly physical activity, older adults should do varied multicomponent physical activity that emphasises functional balance and strength training at moderate or greater intensity, on 3 or more days a week, to enhance functional capacity and to prevent falls

Pregnant and postpartum women:
- At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity throughout the week
- Should include a variety of aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities
- Should limit the amount of time spent being sedentary
- Women who habitually engaged in vigorous-intensity aerobic activity or who were physically active before pregnancy can continue these activities during and after their pregnancy

Adults with chronic conditions or disabilities:
- The same recommendations as for adults and older adults apply
- Should engage in regular physical activity according to their abilities; should consult a healthcare provider if unsure about what types and amounts of physical activity are appropriate for them

Physical Activity and Weight Management

Physical activity in the form of aerobic exercise contributes to energy balance and weight control. Aerobic physical activity that uses large muscle groups, is rhythmical in nature, and can be maintained continuously is most effective for burning calories.

High levels of physical activity (above the minimum recommendations) are needed for weight management. Studies suggest:
- To prevent weight gain: 150–250 minutes per week of moderate-intensity physical activity
- To produce modest weight loss: more than 150 minutes per week
- To produce clinically significant weight loss: more than 225–420 minutes per week
- To maintain significant weight loss: 200–300 minutes per week or more

This highlights an important distinction: the minimum recommendations (150 minutes/week) are designed primarily to support general health and disease prevention, not necessarily weight loss. Those with specific weight loss goals may need substantially more activity.

Types of Physical Activity

Aerobic activity: Activities that increase heart rate and breathing, using large muscle groups repetitively and rhythmically. Examples include walking, running, cycling, swimming, dancing, and team sports.

Muscle-strengthening: Activities that make muscles work against resistance. Examples include lifting weights, using resistance bands, bodyweight exercises, yoga, and heavy gardening.

Balance activities: Activities that challenge postural stability and reduce fall risk. Examples include tai chi, yoga, balance board exercises, and single-leg standing exercises.

Flexibility activities: Activities that improve the range of motion of joints. Examples include stretching exercises and yoga.

Reducing Sedentary Behaviour

Sedentary behaviour is any waking behaviour characterised by low energy expenditure in a sitting, reclining, or lying posture. Extended periods of sedentary behaviour are associated with adverse health outcomes independent of physical activity levels.

Even breaking up sitting time with light activity or standing has metabolic benefits. WHO recommends that all adults and older adults limit the amount of time spent sedentary, and replace sedentary time with physical activity of any intensity.
